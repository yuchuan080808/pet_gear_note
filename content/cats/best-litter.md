## Buying Guide

### Scented vs. Unscented Formulas
Cats have highly sensitive noses, and what smells like a fresh linen breeze to you can feel like a chemical assault to them. **If a cat suddenly avoids their box, heavily perfumed litter is a primary suspect.** Unscented litters rely on carbon, baking soda, or quick absorption to trap odors rather than mask them, making them a safer baseline for picky felines.

### Clumping Strength and Maintenance
Weak clumps shatter when scooped. This leaves dirty, urine-soaked fragments behind that ruin the rest of the batch and generate lingering smells. Hard-clumping bentonite clay generally offers the tightest seals, which is especially important if you use [Self-Cleaning Litter Boxes](/cats/best-self-cleaning-litter-boxes/) that rely on mechanical sifting to separate waste. 

### Dust and Respiratory Safety
Even litters advertised as "99.9% dust-free" frequently generate airborne particles during the initial pour. **If you or your cat have asthma or respiratory sensitivities, dusty clay can trigger serious irritation.** Consider heavier grain litters, crystal options, or alternative materials like corn if dust is a strict dealbreaker for your household.

### Compatibility with Box Types
Not all litter works in all boxes. Automated machines require hard, low-moisture clumps to prevent jamming their internal rakes. Conversely, specialized [Standard Litter Boxes](/cats/best-standard-litter-boxes/) with sifting pans might require specific granule sizes to work efficiently. Always match the litter material to your specific hardware.
